# Cross-Venue Price Discovery in World Cup Prediction Markets

## 1. Introduction

Prediction markets provide a useful setting for studying how quickly traders respond to new information during live sporting events. On Kalshi and Polymarket, users can buy and sell contracts on the eventual World Cup winner, so goals, penalties, and other match events can change a country’s tournament-winning probability in real time. The two platforms also create a natural experiment: Kalshi is a CFTC-regulated market aimed at U.S. retail traders, while Polymarket is an offshore, crypto-settled platform, yet the contracts represent the same underlying outcome and settle on the same result. This makes differences in adjustment speed more plausibly attributable to market structure rather than to differences in the asset itself. This project compares short-term price movements across the two venues to determine which one reacts first when important match information arrives. The main question is not whether one platform is always faster, but whether price leadership changes depending on the type of market and the informational importance of the event.

To study this, I built a single-clock recorder that sampled both platforms’ order books every 3.7 seconds and recorded the fetch skew for each row. In one hand-checked cycle, the two venues were fetched only 87 milliseconds apart, supporting the claim that leads measured in seconds were not caused by the collection process. I also publicly pre-registered the detection thresholds before each match so that they could not be adjusted after viewing the results. The dataset contains five recordings covering four Round of 16 matches and one quiet-window control. The results show that leadership is better understood as a market regime than as a fixed venue characteristic. Polymarket moved first in both cleanly adjudicated active-book events (n = 2): Mbappé’s penalty for France and Merino’s late goal for Spain. In the thin Mexico longshot market, however, Kalshi repriced alone while Polymarket’s book remained stale for roughly four minutes. In other low-probability contracts, including the United States and Belgium, neither venue reacted because the match events changed the tournament-winning probability by less than one price tick. The study also finds that automatic threshold-based lead estimators can be misleading because ordinary order-book noise may trigger a false signal before genuine information arrives, making direct examination of the raw price traces necessary.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Data collection

I collected data from both venues via their public order-book APIs using a recorder synchronized to a single local clock and running on an approximately 3.7-second cycle. During each cycle, the recorder polled Kalshi and Polymarket and saved the best bid, best ask, midpoint, and spread for each contract in JSONL format. The fetch time for each venue was recorded on every row, allowing the within-cycle skew to be measured directly rather than assumed to be negligible. A single clock is necessary because cross-venue timestamps are comparable only when one time source orders both observations; separate venue clocks could introduce synchronization error that appears to be a price lead.

### 2.2 Pre-registration and event detection

For each contract and cycle, the cross-venue basis was defined as the Kalshi midpoint minus the Polymarket midpoint. Before each kickoff, I recorded a pre-match quiet window, calculated that contract’s baseline mean basis and standard deviation from the observations in that window, and committed the window, snapshot count, statistics, and detection rule to a public GitHub repository. Each commit was publicly timestamped before the match began.

A divergence episode was defined by the following pre-registered rule:

`|basis − mean| > max(4σ, 1¢), sustained for at least 30 seconds`

The fixed one-cent floor was included because a standard-deviation rule alone becomes unstable on unusually quiet order books. The pre-match basis standard deviation was only 0.036¢ for Spain and exactly 0.000¢ for Belgium. In those cases, a pure 4σ rule would classify sub-tick variation as a meaningful event even though the movement would not represent an economically observable repricing. The one-cent minimum therefore does most of the filtering when the pre-match basis is nearly constant, while the 4σ term remains relevant for noisier markets. Committing the baseline and rule before kickoff prevents either from being selected after the price paths are known.

### 2.3 Event logs and onset identification

Match events were recorded in append-only logs containing the broadcast minute and a wall-clock timestamp for every entry except the USA–Belgium match, which was logged by phone at minute-level precision. Corrections were appended as new entries rather than replacing earlier ones, preserving a record of what was known at the time. Logged stamps generally lagged the first market response by roughly 20–70 seconds because of broadcast delay and typing latency. The 36′ and 37′ entries, for example, are only 21 seconds apart in wall-clock time, demonstrating that displayed broadcast minutes cannot be treated as exact event clocks. The logs therefore label the likely cause of a movement, while the market data define when that movement began.

For each candidate event, onset was identified by scanning both venues symmetrically for the first price movement consistent with the direction of the new information. The same procedure was applied to Kalshi and Polymarket, so the onset definition did not mechanically privilege either venue. Raw bid, ask, midpoint, and spread traces were then examined around the event window to determine whether one venue moved first, whether the second venue followed, or whether only one venue repriced.

### 2.4 Data-integrity checks

Each dataset was checked for valid JSON parsing, balanced row structure across the recorded contracts, and abnormal sampling gaps. Gaps were cross-referenced against the event logs to determine whether missing observations overlapped with a relevant match event. All five datasets passed these checks. One filename difference requires clarification: the Mexico–England pre-registration refers to `cross_venue_mexico_england.jsonl`, while the analysis uses `cross_venue_mexico_england_main.jsonl`. The `_main` file was created after a collector restart and contains the recording used for the final analysis; the change reflects a restart rather than a different detection rule or a post hoc substitution of datasets.

## 3. Committed Results

The pre-registered detector identified 31 divergence episodes across the four recorded matches. It produced zero false positives during the quiet-window control, a period known to contain no match events. The 30-second sustain requirement was conservative: several visually clear event-driven divergences missed the cutoff by roughly one second because the venues re-coupled rapidly. Sustained cross-venue decoupling therefore rarely survived for more than approximately 30 seconds even during live play, providing a direct measure of how quickly arbitrage pressure reconnected the books.

The detected episodes did not represent a single type of behavior. Some were brief shocks tied to identifiable match events, while others were slower movements in the basis. The clearest example of the second category was a Portugal–Spain episode that lasted approximately 22 minutes and reflected gradual drift rather than a discrete information shock. The results therefore separate event-driven repricing from longer-lived basis drift instead of treating every threshold crossing as evidence of one venue leading the other.

USA–Belgium provides the clearest null case. Despite five goals, the detector found no sustained decoupling. Around the 56′ goal, Polymarket printed 1.85¢ on every row of the ±120-second window, while Kalshi remained within its ordinary approximately ±0.05–0.10¢ band. The only visible disturbance was a single-row −0.25¢ Kalshi midpoint excursion that immediately reverted. The goals changed the approximately 2¢ tournament-winning contracts by less than one tick, so there was no economically observable repricing for either venue to lead.

## 4. Lead Attribution

### 4.1 Failure of the automated estimators

I first attempted to assign price leadership using two automated estimators. Both produced the wrong verdict for the clearest event in the dataset. For Mbappé’s penalty, the first estimator reported that Kalshi led by 156.5 seconds, while the second reported a Kalshi lead of 36.9 seconds. Direct inspection of the order-book rows showed the opposite: Polymarket made the first information-consistent upward move, while Kalshi remained flat before adjusting several seconds later.

The 156.5-second verdict is directly explained by the trace timestamps. The estimator selected a routine Kalshi fluctuation at 15:27:30.316 as its trigger, while Polymarket’s true repricing began at approximately 15:30:06.8; subtracting those timestamps produces the reported 156.5-second “lead.” The estimate was therefore the distance between an unrelated pre-event quote change and the actual information arrival.

The first estimator’s error followed from four concrete settings. Its `MOVE_THRESHOLD` was 0.5¢, small enough for ordinary Kalshi book noise to clear by itself. Its 60-second lookback allowed gradual pre-event drift to qualify as a movement, and its 120-second search window reached far enough backward to capture fluctuations unrelated to the event. Finally, the onset anchor was itself contaminated by the same noisy series. Together, these choices rewarded the first threshold crossing rather than the first information-consistent repricing.

A separate Kalshi artifact caused an additional failure. Roughly 40 seconds before the goal, the Kalshi midpoint dipped by −1.5¢ in the wrong direction for the coming information and then began to reverse. This dip did not create the 156.5-second first-crossing verdict; instead, it dragged the episode’s onset anchor earlier and widened the search region in the wrong direction. The trace therefore documents two distinct mechanisms: a routine 0.5¢ fluctuation selected as the first crossing and a later, larger wrong-direction dip that contaminated the onset anchor.

These failures show that a threshold crossing cannot be interpreted as information arrival without considering direction and context. A noisier or more frequently updating venue will be favored mechanically by a rule that rewards whichever series moves first. Raising the threshold, adding smoothing, or requiring persistence changes which fluctuation is selected but does not remove the underlying bias. Automated first-crossing estimators are therefore noise-biased toward the more active venue in this setting.

### 4.2 Manual adjudication procedure

Because the automated methods failed on an event whose ordering was clear in the raw data, final lead attribution was based on manual adjudication of the recorded order-book traces. For each candidate event, I examined both venues symmetrically and identified the first movement consistent with the information entering the market. A movement qualified as information-consistent only when its direction matched the event’s effect on the contract: favorable news should increase the affected country’s tournament-winning price, while unfavorable news should decrease it. Bid, ask, midpoint, and spread were considered together so that a temporary midpoint change caused by an isolated quote could be distinguished from a genuine repricing of the book.

The procedure distinguished among three possible outcomes. A venue was classified as leading only when it repriced first and the other venue subsequently moved in the same informational direction. A venue was classified as repricing alone when the second book remained stale rather than following. When neither venue made a meaningful information-consistent move, the event was classified as a null rather than forcing a lead verdict. Ambiguous traces were downgraded instead of being counted as evidence for either platform.

### 4.3 Why VECM and Hasbrouck information shares were not used

I also considered estimating price discovery through a vector error-correction model and Hasbrouck information shares. Those methods are designed for longer, continuously adjusting price series with a stable cointegrating relationship. The present data instead consist of short event windows, discrete price ticks, long runs of unchanged quotes, thin books, and occasional one-venue staleness. More importantly, only two active-book events produced clean, manually verified lead-follow sequences. The measured Merino lead of approximately 3.6 seconds is itself at the resolution of the approximately 3.7-second collection cycle, so a model decomposing information shares at finer granularity would manufacture precision the data cannot contain. I therefore use the narrower claim supported by the evidence: event-level ordering can be identified in a small number of clear traces, but the dataset is not sufficient to estimate a general structural information share for either venue.

## 5. Price-Discovery Regimes

The evidence does not identify one venue as consistently faster. Instead, lead attribution depends on the pre-event condition of the two order books and on whether the incoming information is large enough to produce a tradable price change. Regime membership is determined from observable conditions before the outcome is known rather than from which venue ultimately appears to win. Before their events, the France contract at approximately 32¢ and the Spain contract at approximately 10¢ had actively updating quotes on both venues; Polymarket’s Mexico book was already visibly stale before the 43′ goal; and the USA and Belgium contracts were trading at levels where the effect of a single goal could be smaller than the one-cent tick.

Three regimes appear in the adjudicated traces. In the first regime, both books were active and the event clearly changed the contract’s value. Polymarket, the offshore venue, moved first in both clean lead-follow cases (n = 2): Mbappé’s penalty and Merino’s late goal. Kalshi incorporated the same information within one to three sampling cycles, or approximately 3.7–11 seconds. In the second regime, one book was thin or stale. Following Mexico’s 43′ goal, Kalshi, the regulated venue, repriced the Mexico contract alone while Polymarket remained near 2¢ for approximately four minutes. This was not a conventional lead because the second venue did not follow. In the third regime, the information content of the event was smaller than the contract’s minimum price increment. The USA and Belgium contracts were both trading near 2¢, and even five match goals did not change their World Cup-winning probabilities enough to produce a one-tick adjustment. In this regime, no observable repricing existed for either venue to lead.

The contrast between Mexico and Belgium shows that these regimes are not merely price-level buckets. Both contracts traded in the same approximately 2¢ neighborhood, but the events carried different information. Mexico’s 43′ goal reduced a 0–2 deficit to 1–2 and materially changed its probability of surviving a knockout match, producing a tournament-outright value change larger than one tick. Belgium’s 56′ goal extended a match whose outcome was already largely decided, so its effect on an approximately 2¢ World Cup-winning probability remained below one tick. The same longshot price level can therefore fall into the stale-book regime or the no-repricing regime depending on what the event is worth.

The Kalshi response following Mbappé’s penalty also provides evidence about the mechanism behind delayed adjustment. Rather than jumping immediately to the new price, Kalshi moved through a staircase of smaller changes over approximately 4–11 seconds while its spread widened to 1.5¢. This pattern is consistent with maker withdrawal as categorized in the companion `kalshi-mm-study`: liquidity providers first pull back or widen their quotes when exposed to new information, and the midpoint reaches its new level only as the book is rebuilt. The delay therefore does not necessarily indicate that Kalshi traders remained unaware of the event. It may instead reflect a temporary reduction in displayed liquidity while market makers reassessed the contract’s value.

## 6. Limitations

The main limitation is the small number of adjudicated events. The study contains approximately six event-level cases, but only two are clean lead-follow observations in which both venues repriced and the ordering could be identified. The findings therefore do not support a general claim that either platform is systematically faster. Timing precision is also limited by the approximately 3.7-second collection cycle. Any lead shorter than one cycle cannot be measured reliably, and the Merino result—one venue moving at 13:52:45.440 and the other at 13:52:49.020—sits almost exactly at that resolution floor. It establishes ordering within the sampled rows — corroborated by the mirrored Portugal-contract response in the same rows — but not a precise sub-second or within-cycle lead.

The contracts themselves also limit the size of observable responses. These were tournament-outright contracts rather than match moneylines, so the effect of an individual goal was filtered through the country’s probability of surviving the match and eventually winning the World Cup. A paired match-winner market would likely produce larger and more frequent price changes, particularly for events occurring in matches involving longshot teams. Lead attribution was also performed manually by one reader, which introduces judgment into decisions about whether a move was information-consistent. To reduce that risk, every verdict was independently re-derived from the raw bid, ask, midpoint, and spread traces rather than inherited from the initial event labels, some of which originated from informal late-night screenshot review. That verification pass changed the evidence set: the Mexico–England mirror leg was downgraded when its ordering could not be supported cleanly.

The event logs were not accurate enough to serve as market clocks. Broadcast delay and logging latency placed recorded event times roughly 20–70 seconds after the first market response, and the USA–Belgium log was recorded at minute-level precision by phone. The logs are therefore used only to label events; onset timing comes from the market traces. In addition, the total of 31 committed-layer episodes is output from a single detection script. The underlying trace-extraction pipeline was checked against raw rows, but the episode-counting script did not receive the same independent event-by-event validation. Those counts should be treated as descriptive context rather than as support for the headline lead-attribution results.

Finally, the evidence comes from one tournament, collected over roughly one fortnight, and compares only Kalshi with Polymarket. The observed regimes may depend on the platforms’ user bases, liquidity conditions, contract specifications, and market-making behavior during this particular period. A larger study would need more tournaments, additional venue pairs, higher-frequency collection, and more events within each pre-defined book-state regime before estimating how often each type of price-discovery behavior occurs.
